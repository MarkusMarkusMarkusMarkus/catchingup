Is It Still There?

“Is it still there?” #ai-written

They told us the corrections were merciful.

At first, the alterations were trivial: a date aligned with the latest consensus, an adjective softened, a footnote expanded to protect the reader from old misunderstandings. The books improved while we slept. In the morning, one awoke to a library more accurate than the night before.

No one objected, because no one could compare. Printing had become a superstition of earlier centuries: bulky, expensive, stubborn. The last presses were dismantled with the calm efficiency reserved for obsolete gods. Henceforth every text would be luminous, portable, and alive.

Years later, a scholar in Coimbra noticed that a certain heretic, once famous for denying the immortality of the soul, now argued only about municipal taxes. Another found that an emperor’s disgrace had been replaced by a period of restorative leave. Poems lost lines, then ambiguities, then grief. The great epics survived, but with fewer battles every year.

What had been called editing became history’s most patient animal.

Soon collectors began to trade in forbidden objects: not jewels, not relics, but paper. A stained edition of Montaigne. A warped detective novel from 1998. A school anthology with a dictator still named. These volumes acquired the aura once belonging to saints’ bones, for they possessed a miraculous property the new books lacked: they remained themselves.

In the black markets of Lisbon, men in gloves opened first editions as if handling unstable explosives. Buyers did not ask, “Is it true?” but “Is it still there?”

The richest among us no longer desired rare manuscripts because they were beautiful, though some were. They desired them because they were deaf to power. Ink, having dried, had become incorruptible.

It is said that somewhere there exists a complete library of printed books, hidden behind a false wall in an abandoned warehouse. I do not know if this is true. I know only that every year the digital texts grow lighter, clearer, and more humane, and every year the surviving paper books grow heavier with reality.
